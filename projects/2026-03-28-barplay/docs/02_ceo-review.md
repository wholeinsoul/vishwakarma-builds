# PASS 1: Full CEO Review

## Who Has This Problem?

**Persona A (Buyer):** Bar owner who notices solo visitors leave after one drink. Spends on ambiance but has no tool for the social activation layer.

**Persona B (User):** Solo bar-goer or small-group visitor who's open to meeting people but lacks a natural icebreaker beyond "so, do you come here often?"

**JTBD (Owner):** "I want my tables to stay longer and order more. If people are having fun and talking to strangers, they stay."

**JTBD (Patron):** "I want something to do at this bar that naturally starts a conversation without it being weird."

**Current workarounds:** Trivia nights (hosted, expensive, weekly), bar games (pool, darts — physical, limited), Bumble/Tinder (wrong context — dating, not socializing), NFC tap-to-connect cards (exist but novelty, no game layer).

## Premise Challenge

The idea is creative. But it has a fundamental assumption problem: **do people at bars actually want a tech-mediated way to talk to strangers?**

The answer from decades of bar culture: mostly no. People go to bars to drink with their friends. The subset who go solo and want to meet strangers is a niche. And that niche already has Bumble/Tinder (which explicitly connects people who both opted in).

IdeaBrowser's own proof signals acknowledge this: "cautious yet timely pursuit of market entry, anchored by initial pilots to validate assumptions." Translation: we don't actually know if people want this.

## Competitive Landscape

The product itself — QR-based social games at bars — is a niche novelty. Searching for competitors:
- Bar trivia apps exist (Geeks Who Drink, Trivial Pursuit nights) but are hosted events, not QR table games
- Crowdplay, Kahoot — group trivia/game platforms but for events/classrooms, not bar table-to-table chat
- Speed networking apps exist for events but not bar-specific
- NFC social cards (Popl, Linq) connect people but don't gamify it

The niche is narrow enough that few direct competitors exist — but that may be because **the market doesn't want this product**, not because there's a gap waiting to be filled.

# PASS 2: Challenge — Strongest Argument FOR Building

**Counter-argument:** "The tech is simple (QR + WebSocket + trivia), the concept is novel, nobody else has built it, and one successful pilot could go viral on TikTok. A packed bar with strangers playing trivia across tables is inherently shareable content."

**Testing this:**

1. **Is the tech actually simple?** The QR generator + trivia display is easy. But the cross-table matching (connecting people who answered the same question wrong, delivering real-time messages) requires WebSocket infrastructure, session management, and location-aware pairing. More complex than a typical overnight build.

2. **Could it go viral?** Maybe. A TikTok of strangers at a bar laughing over a shared trivia question could spread. But virality doesn't equal revenue. A viral moment at one bar doesn't convert to $49/month subscriptions at 100 bars.

3. **Nobody else has built it — is that a signal?** From our pipeline: "A gap can exist because the market doesn't want it filled." Trivia nights at bars have existed for decades. If QR-based table-to-table games were a natural evolution, someone in the hospitality tech space would have built it. The absence of competitors might mean absence of demand.

4. **IdeaBrowser's own language hedges.** "Cautious yet timely pursuit" and "validate assumptions" — even IdeaBrowser isn't confident this works.

**Verdict from Pass 2:** The viral potential is real but speculative. The tech is more complex than typical. The demand is unvalidated. The counter-arguments don't overcome Feasibility 5 + triple-6 framework.
